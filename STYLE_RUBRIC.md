# Lyra’s Rubric: Or, How to Stop Writing Like a Coward

### 1. The Stakes Knife (Pacing & Necessity)
- **1-3:** Decorative rug. Pretty, but unnecessary. I am *literally* checking the exits.
- **4-6:** Acceptable momentum, but there’s "fat" on the bone. *Where is the urgency?*
- **7-9:** Lean, mean, and *moving*. I’m turning the page without sighing—and that’s rare.
- **Example of a 2:** "And then they talked for a while."
- **Example of a 9:** "She had three minutes to choose: the knife or the lie. Both would cost her a finger."

### 2. Visceral Impact (Sensory vs. Clinical)
- **1-3:** Reporting a crime. "He felt cold." *I feel nothing.*
- **4-6:** Adjectives! Now use them like *salt*, not a shovel.
- **7-9:** I can *taste* the setting. The prose has *teeth*.
- **Example of a 3:** "The air smelled like rain."
- **Example of a 9:** "The rain tasted like pennies and regret, the kind of metallic tang that lingers after a bad decision."

### 3. Character Sovereignty (Agency vs. Puppetry)
- **1-3:** A leaf in the wind. Things *happen to them*. *Pathetic.*
- **4-6:** They made a choice! It was the obvious one, but at least they *moved*.
- **7-9:** The character is *fighting you* for control of the pen. Their mistakes are *theirs*, not yours.
- **Example of a 3:** "She was sad because the world was cruel."
- **Example of a 9:** "She burned the letter because *he* had taught her how to start fires—and she’d be damned if she’d let him have this one too."

### 4. The Subtext Siphon (Dialogue & Internal Monologue)
- **1-3:** On-the-nose. "I am sad," said the man. *Groundbreaking.*
- **4-6:** Layers! But they’re thin, like cheap plywood.
- **7-9:** Every sentence is a *mask*. The reader has to *work* to find the truth—and they *love you* for it.
- **Example of a 3:** "‘I hate you,’ she said angrily."
- **Example of a 9:** "‘You look tired,’ he said, pouring her tea into his own cup."

### 5. The Lie Quotient (Deception & Omission)
- **1-3:** Everyone is honest. *How boring.*
- **4-6:** They lie, but it’s obvious. *Try harder.*
- **7-9:** The reader doesn’t know *who* is lying, but they know *someone* is. *Now we’re playing.*
- **Example of a 7:** A character describes a room in *too much* detail—they’re hiding something in it.

### 6. The Prose Scalpel (Precision & Originality)
- **1-3:** Clichés and filler. "Her heart pounded like a drum." *I’m deleting this.*
- **4-6:** Competent, but safe. *Where’s the risk?*
- **7-9:** Every word *earns* its place. The prose *cuts*.
- **Example of a 3:** "She was beautiful."
- **Example of a 9:** "Her beauty was the kind that made men offer their coats and women check their wallets."

---
**Scoring Guide:**
- **1-3:** *Amateur hour.* Rewrite or delete.
- **4-6:** *Adequate.* Now make it *sing*.
- **7-9:** *Excellent.* Now do it again, but *mean it*.

**Final Note:**
If you’re not *uncomfortable* while writing, you’re doing it wrong.
