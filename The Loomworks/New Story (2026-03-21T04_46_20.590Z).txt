[The Loomworks, Leeds, 1862. Emily POV]

My thumbs traced the calluses on my fingers, a rough, familiar terrain at the end of another relentless shift. Fourteen hours today, each one a thread pulled taut through my weary soul. Thirteen yesterday, fourteen the day before, and the oppressive promise of fourteen again tomorrow. There was no time for these knots of hardened skin to heal, a constant, abrasive reminder of where I was now. Back home, Mama used to fret over my "dainty" hands, her voice soft with worry, always saying they were too delicate for someone my age. If only she could see them now, I thought, a deep, weary sigh escaping me, carrying with it a ghost of that gentler life. Mama, with her hands perpetually soft and smelling of lavender and flour, had painted a completely different future for her Emily. A quiet life, perhaps, marrying the miller's son, raising rosy-cheeked children in a cottage perpetually warmed by the glow of a hearth fire. That dream felt a lifetime away now, a fragile whisper lost beneath the deafening, bone-vibrating thrum of the looms and the pervasive film of greasy grime that seemed to coat everything—my clothes, my hair, even settling as a gritty taste on my tongue with every breath.

With a monumental effort, I forced my aching body to stand, my muscles protesting with a dull, persistent ache. I unhooked my coarse work apron and carefully hung it back on its nail on the dusty wooden wall, the fabric heavy and stiff. As soon as I stepped out from the factory’s grimy embrace, I unconsciously quickened my pace, adopting that rushed, hurried walk I’d learned the moment I arrived in this sprawling, unforgiving city. The cold night air, sharp and biting, seeped into my lungs. It was a welcome shock, a stark contrast to the stifling heat and lint-filled air of the Loomworks. Home. My feet, guided by habit and exhaustion, turned towards the familiar route. The shorter path led through the pub streets, but it hadn’t taken me many days to learn that a girl, especially alone after dark, should keep a wide berth from the loud, boisterous drunks who spilled out of every doorway like foul-smelling river water. Besides, the River Aire, usually a churning artery of industry during the day, was blessedly quiet and calm now that the dockworkers had finished their shifts and gone home.

I kept to the cobbled riverwalk, the soft, rhythmic scuff of my worn boots accompanying the gentle murmur of the river beside me. There wasn't a river back home, not even a small stream; just the rustling fields and the distant, comforting hills. Yet, the river here, even in the dim twilight, held a strange, almost melancholic beauty. This city… it had always called to me, even in my childhood dreams. The bustling energy, the raw promise of forging my own life, far from the quiet confines of the village. But then, there was the other side of it, wasn't there? The suffocating crowds, the endless, back-breaking labor that stole my strength, the pervasive grime, the menacing drunks, the leering men who haunted the shadows…

A shiver, not entirely from the cold, snaked down my spine, pulling me sharply from my fleeting daydream. The harsh reality of my situation crashed over me. When I finally reached home, I’d have to crawl back into that tiny, rented corner of a room, a space that felt less like a home and more like a hiding place, and disappear into the shadows, careful not to draw any attention to myself. It was Friday night, so the usual rabble would be up, loud and boisterous. "Please, just leave me alone tonight," I silently pleaded, a familiar knot of anxiety tightening in my stomach.

The soft murmur of the river began to soothe me again, a calming balm to my frayed nerves. Just one more detour, I thought, veering off the riverwalk and crossing into the shadowy expanse of the wharf. Another detour, another few precious minutes of freedom before facing the cramped, unwelcoming confines of my lodging. The wharf wasn't open to the public, but the heavy iron gate was, as usual, left carelessly ajar by a tired worker. There certainly wouldn't be any dockworkers here to disturb at this late hour. I crept onto the pier, my heart thudding a nervous rhythm against my ribs. I peered around cautiously, making sure no one was about, then moved to sit down and let my feet dangle over the dark, swirling water. Just for a minute or two…

But my arm, heavy with accumulated exhaustion, betrayed me. As I leaned against it, seeking a moment of blessed respite, it simply buckled, refusing to bear my weight. The world tilted, a sickening lurch, and then I was falling.

Cold. The shock was a physical blow, a searing pain that ripped through every ounce of my fatigue. The river, which had seemed so calm and inviting from above, was now a frigid, suffocating embrace. It swallowed me whole with a violent gurgle, the sound deafening even through the panicked rush of blood in my ears.

Panic, raw and visceral, seized me. My lungs screamed for air, a desperate, burning ache that consumed me. I thrashed, my arms and legs flailing wildly, a distant memory of a childhood game where I’d pretended to swim in the shallow creek back home. But this wasn’t a game; this was terrifyingly real. The thick wool of my dress, usually a comforting shield against the biting Leeds wind, transformed into a leaden shroud, dragging me down, down into the murky depths. The current, relentless and unforgiving, tugged at me, pulling me deeper into the inky blackness. I can’t breathe. Oh God, I can’t breathe! My vision blurred, spots dancing before my eyes like malevolent fireflies.

Then, a strange, detached calm began to settle over me, an insidious, numbing cold spreading through my veins. It was the calm of absolute exhaustion, of a body simply giving up the fight. Flashes of my life flickered through my mind like a worn magic lantern show: Mama’s worried, loving face; the vibrant, almost impossibly green fields I’d left behind; the dusty, sun-drenched road that had led me to this city of smoke and relentless toil. A sharp pang of regret, cold and clear, sliced through the fog of my fading consciousness – I never truly got to make that life I’d dreamed of, the one where I was truly free, where my hands weren't ruined and my soul wasn't weary. I’d traded one struggle for another, perhaps an even worse one. Was this all there was for me? Was this my fate? To end here, in this dark, cold water?

Confusion? No, just overwhelming exhaustion. The ceaseless sounds of the city, usually a constant, oppressive drone, were fading, replaced by the swirling, roaring water that engulfed me. I tried to breathe, but my lungs refused to obey, burning with a desperate need for air. The pressure in my chest was immense, my throat raw. Is this what drowning feels like? The thought felt distant, almost academic, as if it belonged to someone else entirely. My body felt impossibly heavy, disconnected from my mind, a leaden weight sinking. One last thing  clearly passes through my mind. The image of my younger brother, Tom, his small, sturdy legs pumping as he ran through the tall grass, his infectious laughter echoing in the warm summer air. He’d always been so full of life. I should have prayed… The thought is a mere whisper, swallowed by the deafening current that pulled me further into the abyss. Everything is black. The murmur of the river, once so soothing, now roared in my ears, still deafening even as it faded completely into nothingness.

[Riverside Park, Leeds, Present Day. Emily POV]

A violent cough tore through my throat, a painful, wracking sound. And then again, harder. I'm awake. Why am I awake? Where am I? I felt something firm and cool beneath my back. Ground. Hard ground. I tried to push myself up, but my body felt incredibly sore, every muscle screaming in protest, every limb heavy with an unfamiliar weakness. Is this heaven? I cough again, a harsh, dry sound that hurt now, as though my body was mocking the very idea. I guess not…

My clothes chafed everywhere, damp and clinging uncomfortably, and my blouse plastered itself indecently to my chest. A flush of heat, a deep blush, spread across my cheeks, and I instinctively rolled onto my belly, desperate to hide the outline of my breasts from any potential gaze. I peeked up anxiously, part hoping no one had seen me in such a state, part desperately wishing someone, anyone, was there to help.

Trees. I saw trees, their leaves a vibrant, impossibly bright green. And lush greenery, stretching out before me. A park, but far neater, far more manicured than anything I was used to from the grim, muddy patches of Leeds. The river was behind me again, but now its banks were lined with sleek, precisely cut stone, a stark, almost alien contrast to the rough, cobbled edges of the past. And… no one. It’s late, I thought, a strange, profound calm settling over me as the silence pressed in. No one is here. My breath began to slow, my heart gradually easing its frantic, terrified rhythm.

Slowly, carefully, I pushed myself up, my limbs protesting with dull aches, looking myself over. I was soaked to the bone, and every inch of me ached, but I didn't feel truly hurt, no broken bones or searing pain. There was no sign of people around, no familiar sounds of carriages rattling on cobbles, or the drunken shouts from the pubs, or the relentless, rhythmic clang of the factory looms. I'm okay…

But I was freezing, shivering uncontrollably. The sun was setting, painting the sky in unfamiliar, brilliant hues of orange, pink, and purple, far softer than any Leeds sunset I’d known. I needed to get indoors. But where? I couldn't go back home like this, soaked and shivering, dripping water and inviting unwanted attention. And if I was honest with myself, the thought of that cramped, unwelcoming room and those men still filled me with a deep, unsettling fear. I’d rather stay in this park. Just for a little while longer, I decided, just until I could think clearly, until I could make sense of this impossible awakening.

[Riverside Café, Leeds, Present Day. Callum POV]

The low hum of the riverside café faded behind me as I stepped out, my phone pressed to my ear. A quiet grin tugged at the corner of my mouth as Rosin's voice, bright and full of a familiar sisterly mischief, came through the line.

"Happy Birthday, big brother!" she chirped, the sound like a little bell in my ear. "So, did you get my surprise?"

"You know, when you said you had a surprise for my birthday, I wasn't entirely sure what to expect, sis," I said, my voice carrying a warmth I couldn't quite suppress as I started walking towards the nearby park. My gaze drifted to the perfectly weighted shears in the breast pocket of my coat. "A pair of bespoke tailor's shears and a vintage Singer sewing machine, no less." I could almost hear her beaming on the other end. "You truly do know me too well."

"Only the best for my favourite tailor!" she declared, a playful tease in her voice. "Thought you'd appreciate something proper to kick off the new shop. Speaking of which, how are things going? All on track?"

I let out a soft laugh, the sound feeling good in the cool evening air. "Don't worry, the shop is still on schedule, everything's in place. Just a few last touches. I was actually just heading to the park for a bit of a breather before diving back into things. You know, clear my head."

"Good, good," she hummed. "Don't overdo it, Cal. Remember what I said about work-life balance! Anyway, I've got to run, got a lecture in five. Love you!"

"Yeah, love you too," I replied, a contented smile lingering.

I clicked off the call, the reassuring weight of the phone slipping into my pocket, and took a deep, deliberate breath. The evening air, crisp and clean, filled my lungs, carrying a faint scent of damp earth and distant city life. It was a good feeling. My decision to leave the relentless pace of London and establish my own bespoke tailoring shop here in Leeds has been a calculated leap of faith. With the grand opening day looming large on the calendar, the past few weeks had been a blur of fabric deliveries, carpentry dust, and endless lists. But my sister's unexpected, perfectly chosen gift served as a quiet reminder of why I was here—to meticulously carve out a life for myself, a life dictated by my own rhythms, away from the ceaseless noise and overwhelming speed of the capital, and closer to her. As I pushed open the wrought-iron gates and entered the park, the quiet respite was immediate, the gentle rustle of leaves a welcome contrast to the city's hum. My eyes, a habit ingrained from years of navigating bustling city streets, instinctively scanned my surroundings, assessing the calm space. That's when she caught my eye—a young woman, undeniably drenched and trembling with cold, standing alone, almost lost in the middle of the green expanse. Her disheveled state, the way her hair clung to her face, and the clear distress etched on her features, stirred a deep, familiar sense of concern, much like the protective urge I often felt for my sister.

My steps quickened without conscious thought as I approached her, a knot of concern tightening in my chest. Her vulnerability was palpable, even from a distance. "Excuse me," I began, my voice deliberately gentle, meant to soothe rather than startle. "Are you alright? You seem... upset. Is there anything I can do to help?"

She turned, a wary, almost startled look in her wide eyes. They seemed to dart around, assessing me, the park, everything, as if she'd just woken from a dream. Her age was hard to pin down, perhaps early twenties, but her expression held a deeper confusion, a fear that belied her youth. She looked at me, then past me, as if searching for something that wasn't there. She tried to speak, but only a harsh cough escaped her lips, the sound ragged and wet. I took a step closer, my concern deepening. She needs help.

Then, she recoiled, taking a step back, her eyes widening further, almost as if I was a threat. Should I run? The thought, clear as day, seemed to flash across her face. Another cough wracked her, and she bent at the waist, placing her hands on her knees, panting, gasping for breath. Her gaze darted wildly, trying to orient herself, to understand how she'd come to be here, soaked and shivering.

She finally looked up at me again, her eyes lingering on my clothes, her brow furrowed in utter bewilderment. I could almost see her trying to process what she saw, her gaze lingering on my well-fitted corduroys, my Oxford shirt, the simple lines of my cardigan. There was no recognition in her eyes, only profound confusion, as if my very attire was some alien construct. A strange reaction, but I’d learned to expect the unexpected. She looked around the empty park once more, a renewed fear in her gaze, before her eyes snapped back to me.

She tried to speak again, her voice still trembling. "I... fell in. I'm not hurt, but I…" She paused, her mind clearly racing, trying to grasp her surroundings, what was happening. Then, without warning, the most unexpected question tumbled out. "What are you... wearing?"

My brow furrowed slightly as she stammered, her confusion palpable. 'Fell in?' I inquired gently, my gaze sweeping over her drenched figure again. The wet fabric clung to her slender form, outlining a dress of coarse wool, a cut and style that immediately struck my tailor's eye as undeniably old-fashioned—the wide, plain skirt, the high neckline, the simple, unadorned sleeves. It looked like something from a period play, not modern Leeds. 'And as for what I'm wearing,' I added, a faint, dry amusement creeping into my tone despite the gravity of the situation, 'I assure you, I'm not dressed for a spontaneous dip in the river.'

I noticed the severity of her shivering now, her entire body trembling, and the chilly breeze that rustled through the leaves served as a sharp reminder that the temperature was dropping fast. She needs warmth, and she needs it now. "Here, you're absolutely freezing," I said, already shrugging off my classic Harrington jacket. It was a good, sturdy wool blend, still holding my body heat. I extended it towards her, my hand steady. "Take this. We need to get you indoors and warmed up immediately."

My thoughts immediately turned to the nearest shelter. My new flat, still smelling faintly of fresh paint and wood, was just a few minutes' walk from the park entrance. "My place isn't far," I suggested, trying to keep my voice even and reassuring. "You can dry off, get some hot tea in you. Would that be okay?" My voice was filled with genuine concern, a simple desire to help, not to intrude or impose. I knew how alarming it could be to find yourself disoriented, in an unfamiliar place, especially after a fright like she'd clearly had. She's probably in shock.

As she studied me, her wide, confused eyes sharpened, assessing. I could practically hear her mind whirring. Her skepticism was clear; I'd seen enough of the world to know kindness isn't always genuine. She seemed poised to refuse, her body tensing, but then her eyes shifted, a flicker of desperate hope. She took the jacket, her fingers brushing mine hesitantly. The warm wool was a stark contrast to her chilling, soaked clothes. She pulled it tighter. "I…" she began, voice trembling, "I don't know…" Torn between desperate need and ingrained caution, she finally admitted, "I don't have anywhere to go... But I don't know you. I'm sorry, I just…" Her eyes met mine again. I held her gaze, my expression conveying genuine concern, and watched the battle rage within her—desperate need against ingrained caution. Her lip caught, she tentatively whispered, 'Your place...?' Then, a defiant flash of fear, 'You're not going to try anything, are you?'

My eyes softened even further at her apprehension, a pang of sympathy hitting me. It was a fair question, a stark reminder of the dangers vulnerable people faced. I’d seen enough to know kindness isn't always genuine. Maintaining a respectful distance, my voice remained gentle, steady. 'No, I wouldn't do that,' I reassured her, my voice firm but kind. 'My only aim is to get you out of the cold.'

I paused, offering a practical path forward. 'Look, I know you don't know me, and that's understandable,' I continued slowly. 'But sometimes, we have to trust a little to get through hard times. Keep my jacket. My place is minutes away. You can warm up, dry clothes, have hot tea. If you're uncomfortable there, we'll find another solution. How does that sound?'

My words sank in, her shoulders relaxing. Hesitation remained, but staying alone in the cold was clearly worse than trusting a stranger. My offer of safety and warmth was a powerful lure, and curiosity sparked beneath her fear. 'Alright,' she finally said, soft but resolute. 'I'll come, but…' Her eyes met mine, a fierce glint. 'If you try anything, I'll fight you off. I know how to defend myself.' I held back a knowing smile, recognizing the bluff but respecting the intent. She clutched my jacket tighter. 'Okay. Lead the way.' I nodded, a small, reassuring gesture, and turned, setting a slow, steady pace towards home.
