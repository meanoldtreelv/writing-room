# Chapter 4 — The Carriage

I heard it before I saw it.

Not the sound itself — the *wrongness* of the sound. The Vale had its own sounds: the canopy shifting in wind that never reached the ground, the birds arguing in the upper branches, the hum beneath the soil, the well's drip, the distant crack of Gareth's mallet, the even more distant sound of Fern climbing something she shouldn't. These were the sounds of home. They were the sounds I'd heard every day of my life, and they were right, and they fit together the way the village fit together, each one in its place, each one part of the whole.

This sound had no place.

It was wheels. Not cart wheels — the merchant's cart had wheels, wooden, rough, the kind that clattered over roots and groaned on mud and announced themselves from a mile away with the honest sound of something working hard to get where it was going. These wheels were quiet. Not silent — nothing with wheels was ever truly silent — but *muted,* cushioned, the sound of something that had been designed to move without announcing itself. Iron-rimmed, probably. Greased. The kind of wheels that didn't belong on a road made of packed earth and root and the occasional flat stone that someone had laid down decades ago because the mud was bad in that spot.

I was in the garden. The north beds — the Stubborn, the carrots, the second planting of greens. Thorn was beside me, arguing with the soil, which was his version of a conversation. I'd just finished circling the last bed, my finger in the dirt, the groove clean, the symbols settled. The soil was warm. The circles were holding. The carrots were coming in strong and the greens were standing tall and everything was exactly as it should be.

Then the sound.

I looked up. Thorn looked up. Across the garden, Willa straightened from the irrigation channel she was clearing, her hands still full of mud, her eyes going to the sound with the particular alertness of someone who'd lived in the Vale long enough to know what belonged and what didn't. The sound was coming from the south — from the road, if you could call it a road, the packed-earth path that wound through the outer wood and connected the village to the wider world in theory and to the merchant in practice and to nothing else because nothing else came here.

Nothing else was supposed to come here.

"The hell is that," Thorn said, which was as close to alarm as Thorn ever got. Thorn didn't swear. Thorn didn't raise his voice. Thorn argued with the soil and named his beds and was the steadiest man in the village. If Thorn said "the hell," something was wrong.

I didn't answer. I was watching the tree line where the path met the outer wood, where the normal-sized oaks gave way to the great trunks of the Vale, where the canopy thickened and the light changed and the world shifted from ordinary to something else. The sound was closer now. Wheels on packed earth. The particular rhythm of a vehicle that was too well-made for the road it was on, bouncing on roots it wasn't designed for, its suspension — if it had suspension, and the merchant said rich people's carriages had suspension, though I didn't know what that meant beyond "less bouncy" — working harder than it should.

It came through the tree line.

The carriage was polished. That was the first thing I noticed, and it was wrong, and the wrongness hit me before the details did. Wood — dark, oiled, gleaming — that had never seen mud without being scrubbed, never seen rain without being dried, never seen a scratch without being mended. The wheels were iron-rimmed and greased and silent on the packed earth, which explained the muted sound but not the wrongness. The horses — two of them, matched, chestnut, their coats brushed to a shine that the Vale's filtered light turned into something almost metallic — pulled the carriage with the bored efficiency of animals that had been trained to do this and didn't care where they were going.

On the side of the carriage, painted in gold that was too bright for the green light of the Vale, was a symbol I didn't recognize. A circle — multiple rings, interlocking, precise. The kind of circle I drew in the dirt, but different. Cleaner. Colder. The kind of circle that had been drawn by someone who measured rather than felt.

The village gathered.

Not because anyone called them. Not because Torv sent word. The village gathered the way a body responds to a wound — automatically, instinctively, everyone arriving at the same place at the same time because the wrongness was in the air and the village could feel it the way it could feel a storm coming or a frost settling or Grandmother shifting in her sleep.

They came from the hall, from the houses, from the garden, from the granary. Senna first, wiping her hands on her apron, her face carrying the expression of a woman who had just been interrupted and was deciding whether to be annoyed or concerned. Thorn beside me, his hands still in the soil. Willa from the irrigation channels, mud on her arms, silent, watching. Corwin from the herb garden, Hesta beside him, Pax behind them both, his eyes wide with the particular excitement of a twelve-year-old who sensed something interesting was happening. Moss appeared from the tree line the way Moss always appeared — without transition, without sound, as if the forest had simply decided he should be here and he'd agreed. Dara was already talking, low, to Brenna, who was listening with her hands still full of bark fibers from the weaving she'd been doing. Fern dropped from a tree — of course she was in a tree — landing beside me with the silent precision of someone who'd been landing from trees her whole life and had gotten very good at it.

Gareth came from his workshop, his hands full of wood shavings. Lief behind him, quiet, steady. Ailsa came last, or maybe she was already there, because Ailsa was always where she needed to be, the way a stone is always in the river, immovable, present, watching.

Torv walked to meet the carriage.

He walked the way Torv always walked — planted, heavy, rooted. But his hands were doing something they didn't usually do. His fingers were curling and uncurling at his sides, not fists, not trembling, just... moving. Working. The way his hands worked when he was counting in his head, or remembering something he'd rather forget, or carrying a weight that was too big for his shoulders but too important to put down.

The carriage stopped. The horses stamped. The dust settled.

The door opened.

---

The assessor was not what I expected, though I didn't know what I expected because I'd never expected anything because nobody had told me an assessor was coming because nobody in the village knew what an assessor was.

He stepped down from the carriage with the careful precision of a man who was used to stepping down from things — carriages, platforms, stages. He was younger than Torv, older than Bran. His robes were clean — not just clean, *immaculate,* the kind of clean that meant someone had cleaned them for him, the kind of clean that had no relationship to mud or work or the business of living. The fabric was fine. I didn't know fabrics — Brenna knew fabrics, Brenna could tell you the weave and the weight and the fiber from a touch — but even I could see that this cloth had never been anywhere near a bark-stripping rack.

His eyes were careful. Not kind, not cruel, not suspicious, not friendly. *Careful.* The way Corwin's eyes were careful when he examined a new herb — cataloging, assessing, filing away. He looked at the village the way you'd look at a room you'd been told about but never visited, noting what matched the description and what didn't.

He had a letter. Sealed, heavy, the wax bearing the same interlocking-circle symbol as the carriage. He held it in one hand. In the other, he held a crystal.

The crystal was clear. Not diamond — the merchant had shown us diamonds once, small ones, and they'd been bright and hard and cold. This crystal was *warm.* Not warm to the touch — I couldn't feel it from here — but warm in the way it caught the Vale's filtered light and held it and turned it into something that glowed from inside, like a firefly, like an ember, like a heartbeat made visible.

"Elder Torv," the assessor said. His voice was precise. Not the village's way of speaking — practical, communal, everyone talking at once because silence was just a pause between sentences. His voice was *measured.* Each word placed. Each syllable deliberate. "I am Assessor Aldric, by authority of the Crown. I carry the Assessment Mandate." He held up the letter. "Your village has been selected for Gifted Appraisal."

Torv looked at the letter. He looked at the crystal. He looked at the assessor's robes and his polished boots and his careful eyes. His fingers curled and uncurled.

"We know the drill," Torv said.

His voice was steady. His hands were not.

---

The test was simple.

The assessor set up in the village green — the packed-earth space between the houses where the children played and the old ones sat and the meetings happened when something needed deciding. He placed the crystal on a stand he'd brought from the carriage — metal, folding, precise. The crystal sat on the stand like a jewel in a setting, catching the light, glowing from within.

"The Mandate requires assessment of all children of age," the assessor said. He unrolled a list — parchment, not the rough paper the village made from bark. "I will call names. Each child will place their hand on the crystal. The crystal will respond to Gifted potential. If it responds, I will ask questions. If it doesn't, you may step aside."

The village children lined up. There weren't many — the village was small, and "of age" meant old enough to have the gift manifest, which was different for everyone but usually happened in the teens. Pax was twelve and too young and he knew it and he was disappointed. Sage was seven and too young and he was relieved. Rowan was ten and too young and she didn't care. The ones who were old enough — a handful, maybe six, including me — stood in a line that the assessor had organized with the quiet efficiency of someone who had done this many times.

He'd been to a dozen villages. He'd found nothing. His face said so — not in words, but in the particular patience of a man who had learned to expect nothing and was rarely disappointed.

The first child touched the crystal. Nothing. The crystal didn't change. The light inside it didn't shift. The assessor made a note. The child stepped aside.

The second. Nothing.

The third. Nothing.

The fourth. Nothing.

The assessor's face didn't change. He'd been doing this a long time. His patience was not the village's patience — not the patience of Thorn waiting for carrots or Corwin waiting for poultices or Pell waiting for winter. His patience was the patience of a bureaucrat who had a job to do and did it regardless of results because the results were not the point. The point was the process. The point was the mandate. The point was the Crown.

The fifth. Nothing.

The sixth. Nothing.

Then me.

I didn't want to go. Not because I was afraid — there was nothing to be afraid of, it was just a crystal, just a test, just a man in clean robes asking us to touch a stone. I didn't want to go because the line was ending and the village was watching and the assessor was watching and his careful eyes had been cataloging all morning and I didn't want to be cataloged. I didn't want to be a thing on a list. I didn't want to be a result.

Della's hand on my back. Gentle. Pushing.

I walked to the crystal.

It was warm. The crystal was warm, not from the sun — the Vale's light was too filtered to warm anything — but from inside, from the glow I'd seen from across the green, the ember-heart of the stone pulsing with something that felt like the hum beneath the soil, like the warmth in the hearthstone, like the song in my throat.

I placed my hand on the crystal.

It screamed.

Not with sound. Not with light. With *warmth.* The crystal went from warm to hot in a breath — a single, sudden surge, like a fire catching, like a heartbeat slamming into rhythm, like a voice that had been waiting to speak finally opening its mouth. The glow inside the crystal blazed — not bright, not blinding, but *deep,* a warmth that radiated through my palm and up my arm and into my chest and settled there, in the place where the hum lived, the place where the song lived, the place where the soil's pulse lived.

The assessor's quill stopped.

He was looking at me. His careful eyes had changed. The patience was gone. The cataloging was gone. What was left was something I didn't recognize — not surprise, not excitement, not greed. Something older. Something like a man who had been looking for a word in a language he thought was dead and had just heard someone speak it.

He looked at the crystal. He looked at me. He looked at the crystal again.

"How long," he said, and his voice was different now, the precision gone, the measure gone, replaced by something raw, something almost hungry, "have you been casting without a chant?"

I stared at him.

"Casting?"

---

He asked to speak with Torv. Privately. In the shared hall. The rest of the village was dismissed with a nod and a wave that was polite and firm and meant *leave now.*

I didn't leave. I went back to the garden. The carrots were still coming in. The greens were still standing. The circles were still holding. The soil was still warm. Everything was the same. Nothing was the same.

Fern found me at the north beds, sitting in the dirt, my hand on the soil, not casting, not tending, just sitting. She dropped beside me the way she always dropped — from above, from somewhere, from the particular place Fern existed in the world, which was slightly above and slightly to the left of everyone else.

"What happened?"

"I don't know."

"What did the crystal do?"

"It got warm."

"How warm?"

"Very."

"What did the man say?"

"He asked how long I'd been casting without a chant."

"What did you say?"

"I said 'casting?'"

Fern was quiet for a moment. Fern was never quiet. The silence was heavier than anything she could have said.

"Are you in trouble?" she asked.

"I don't know."

"Should we be worried?"

"I don't know."

She put her hand on mine. Her hand was muddy and cold and alive and Fern's. We sat in the dirt by the garden beds and didn't say anything for a while. The carrots grew. The circles held. The soil was warm.

Behind us, in the shared hall, the assessor was talking to Torv. We couldn't hear the words. We could hear the tone — careful, precise, the assessor's voice; heavy, reluctant, Torv's. The conversation went on for a long time. Longer than a test result should take. Longer than a village assessment should take.

When Torv came out of the hall, his face was the same face he always wore — steady, watching, carrying. But his hands were curling and uncurling again. And his eyes, when they found me across the garden, held something I'd never seen in them before.

Not worry. Worry I knew. Worry was Mira's face, Della's face, the face of people who cared about you and were afraid for you.

This was heavier than worry. This was the look of a man who had seen this before and knew what came next and couldn't stop it.

---

The assessor left at dusk. The carriage pulled away through the tree line, its polished wheels finding the path, its iron rims silent on the packed earth. The horses' coats caught the last of the filtered light and shone like copper, like something expensive, like something that didn't belong here and knew it.

He left a letter.

Torv read it in the shared hall, standing, his voice steady, his hands shaking. The village gathered. Not the way they'd gathered when the carriage arrived — not automatically, not instinctively. Slowly. Reluctantly. The way you gather when you know the news is bad but you need to hear it anyway.

The Crown Mandate. Brin was to report to Westmark College. By order of the Crown. No choice. No refusal. The assessment had identified Gifted potential. The law required Gifted individuals to present for education at the Crown's designated institution. The assessor would return in one month to escort her.

The hall was quiet when Torv finished reading.

Nobody said anything.

I looked around the room. Thorn's face was closed, the way it closed when a bed failed and he had to accept it. Willa was still, the way she was still when she was thinking. Corwin's face was doing the complicated thing — respect and resentment and something else, something that might have been recognition, the look of a man who had always known this would happen and was watching it happen and couldn't decide if he was right or wrong. Hesta's hand was on Corwin's arm, grounding him. Pax was staring at me with wide eyes and the expression of a twelve-year-old who didn't understand what was happening but understood that it mattered.

Moss was silent, which was not unusual, but his silence had a different weight tonight — heavier, denser, the silence of a man who spent days in the forest and knew what it sounded like when the forest was afraid. Dara was not talking. Dara was never not talking. Tonight she was not talking and that was the loudest sound in the room.

Fern was beside me. Her hand found mine in the dark. She squeezed. Hard. The way Willa squeezed shoulders. The way the village communicated when words weren't enough.

Senna was at the hearth, not cooking, just standing, her hands on the iron hook where the pot hung, holding it the way Torv held the table — as if the thing she was touching might float away if she let go.

Ailsa was at the head of the table. She was watching me. Her eyes were the same — fierce, immovable, carrying every story she'd ever memorized. She said nothing. She didn't need to. Her face said: *I know what this is. I've seen this before. And I'm sorry.*

Gareth was in the doorway, his hands full of wood shavings he'd forgotten to put down. Brenna was beside him, and Lief behind them, and the three of them stood there like a wall, like a family, like people who were trying to hold something up with their bodies because their words weren't strong enough.

Mira was crying. Not loudly — Mira didn't cry loudly, she cried the way she worried, quietly, constantly, as if the tears were just another form of the anxiety she carried like Pell carried the granary. Pell was beside her, his arm around her, his face blank, his eyes counting something invisible.

Torv put the letter down. He looked at me. His eyes held the weight I'd been seeing my whole life — the weight I'd never been able to name, the weight that sat on his shoulders like a stone he'd been carrying since before I was born.

He was carrying it now. Heavier than ever.

"Brin," he said.

Just my name. Just the two syllables. The way he said it — heavy, deliberate, each letter costing something — I knew there was more. I knew he had something to tell me. I knew it would change everything.

But not tonight. Tonight the village just sat with it. The letter on the table. The crystal's warmth fading. The assessor's carriage disappearing into the outer wood.

I went to the garden one last time before bed. The soil was warm. The circles were holding. The carrots didn't know anything had changed. The greens were still standing tall. The earth under my hand was the same earth it had been this morning — warm, alive, holding.

I pressed my palm flat.

The pulse was there. Slow. Deep. Like a heartbeat. Like a breath.

Like Grandmother was still listening.

I went inside.

I put my hand on the hearthstone. Warm. Always warm.

I closed my eyes.

The village slept. But tonight the forest held it tighter.
