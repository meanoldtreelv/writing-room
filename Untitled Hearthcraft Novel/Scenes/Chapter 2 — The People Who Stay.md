# Chapter 2 — The People Who Stay

The shared hall decided what the village ate the way the village decided everything else — not by voting, not by arguing, but by the simple process of Senna standing at the hearth in the morning and looking at what was available and making the decision that everyone else would agree with because Senna had been making that decision for thirty years and she was always right. Today it was root stew, because the north beds had come in strong and the cellar had turnips that needed using before they went soft. Nobody argued. Nobody ever argued with Senna about food. You could argue with Senna about the weather, about the merchant's prices, about whether the eastern path was shorter than the western path. You did not argue with Senna about dinner.

Thorn came in from the fields at midmorning, boots caked with the dark soil of the garden beds, his face carrying the particular expression of a man who had been arguing with the earth since dawn and was winning, barely. He dropped into his seat at the long table and pulled the bread toward him without looking — his hands knew where it was, the way they knew where every tool in his belt was, the way they knew the soil. Willa came in behind him, silent as always, her shoulders carrying the weight of a morning spent hauling water from the well to the irrigation channels that ran between the beds. She squeezed Senna's shoulder on the way past — hard, the way Willa did everything — and Senna squeezed back without turning around, a conversation conducted entirely in pressure.

I watched them from the herb table in the corner where I was sorting the dried thyme that Dara had brought in from the racks. The thyme was good — properly dried, the leaves still green, the stems flexible. Dara's drying operation was the best in the village, which was partly because she was meticulous and partly because Moss spent days in the deep forest finding herbs that nobody else could find and Dara took it personally if they weren't preserved right. Moss was not at the table. Moss was never at the table at midmorning. Moss was in the forest, being quiet at mushrooms.

"Brin." Corwin's voice, from the herb table beside mine. He didn't look up. He never looked up when he said my name — he just said it into whatever he was working on, the way you'd call a tool by name before you used it. "The chamomile. Top shelf. Hand me the bundle with the blue twine."

I reached up and found it. The blue twine was Hesta's marking — she tied different colors for different drying stages so Corwin could tell at a glance how far along a batch was without having to check it himself, which he would have done anyway because Corwin checked everything himself even when he didn't need to. I handed him the bundle. He took it without looking, brought it to his nose, inhaled.

"Good batch," he said. "Dara's getting better at the timing."

"She was already good at the timing."

"She was adequate. Now she's good." He pulled a sprig loose and held it up, examining the flower heads with the precise attention of a man who had spent his life learning to see what other people missed. "This is the batch for the sleep tincture. The one I'm making for Pell."

"Pell doesn't sleep?"

"Pell counts seeds in his head until dawn. He told me it helps him relax." Corwin's face did the thing it always did when he was about to say something dry — a tightening around the eyes, a slight downturn at the mouth. "He needs the tincture."

I almost smiled. Corwin's humor was like his medicine — precise, targeted, and delivered without warmth. He didn't mean it unkindly. He just didn't see the point of wrapping a diagnosis in softness when the patient needed the cure, not the comfort.

He was working on a poultice for the herb garden's border — something to keep the slugs off the lettuce without using circles, because Corwin didn't use circles, not because he couldn't but because he'd spent years trying to learn the songs my grandmother sang and couldn't hear them and had decided that if the gift wouldn't come to him he'd build his own gift out of knowledge and stubbornness and the best damn poultice recipes in the March. I offered to help — not with the poultice, Corwin wouldn't let anyone touch his poultices, but with the border itself. I could circle the lettuce beds the way I circled the tomato beds, and the circles kept voles out, and they'd keep slugs out too if I drew the symbols right.

He looked at me. For a moment his face did the thing it always did when I offered to help with something his herbs could do — a complicated expression that held respect and resentment in the same breath, like two fish trying to swim in opposite directions in the same pond. He respected what I could do. He resented that I could do it without trying while he'd spent forty years learning to do it the hard way. Both feelings were real. Both lived in him at the same time. He never said either one out loud.

"The slugs are my problem," he said. "Your circles are for the food."

"They could be for the border too."

"Then what would I do with my afternoons?" He went back to his poultice. I went back to my thyme. The conversation was over, and it had been the same conversation we'd been having for years — him refusing my help, me offering it, both of us knowing he'd eventually accept, both of us pretending he wouldn't. It was the rhythm of us. Like the pot Senna argued with. Like Torv holding the table down.

Hesta appeared between us the way Hesta always appeared — not physically, but socially, filling the gap that Corwin left when he was being Corwin. She set a cup of tea beside him without being asked — he drank it without thanking her, which was his way of saying thank you — and then turned to me with the calm, steady expression that meant she was about to translate something.

"He spent all morning on that poultice," she said. "He's proud of it. He'll never say so."

"I heard that," Corwin said.

"You were meant to."

Pax, their son, was at the far end of the herb table, pretending to organize dried mint and actually asking me questions with his eyes. Pax was twelve and full of questions the way the well was full of water — constantly, overflowing, and impossible to stop. He didn't ask out loud because Corwin had told him that interrupting a sorting session was "an offense against the natural order of herbology," which was Corwin's way of saying "be quiet while I'm working" and Pax's way of learning that some questions needed to wait. But his eyes were loud. They said: *can you do the thing? the circle thing? can you show me? does it hurt? does it tickle? can you do it to me?*

I'd circled him once, playfully, last summer. Drew a ring around his wrist with my thumb while he wasn't paying attention, hummed a bar of the song. He said he felt warm for the rest of the day. He'd been asking for another one ever since. I hadn't done it again — not because it was wrong, but because Pax didn't need magic. He needed herbs. He needed to learn what his father knew. The gift would come or it wouldn't, and either way he'd be useful, and either way he'd be himself, and that was enough.

He caught my eye and I gave him the smallest nod — *later, maybe, not today* — and he went back to his mint with the satisfied expression of someone who had successfully conducted a negotiation without words.

---

I went to the outer wood after lunch.

Not to find Fern — Fern would find me, Fern always found me, Fern had a sense for where I was that had nothing to do with tracking and everything to do with the fact that Fern paid attention to me the way Della paid attention to the garden, which was to say she noticed when something was different and showed up to ask about it. I went because Gareth was there, and I hadn't checked the north path in a week, and the north path needed checking because the spring rains had been heavy and the roots shifted when they were wet and a path that was clear last week might have a fallen branch across it this week.

Gareth was at his workshop — not a building, because the village didn't build buildings the way the merchant described cities building them, all straight lines and hard corners. Gareth's workshop was a space between three roots, roofed by living bark, walled by the trees themselves. He'd asked the roots where they'd allow walls, and the roots had shown him, and the walls fit perfectly because Gareth listened and the forest answered.

He was shaping a chair. Not a fancy chair — the village didn't do fancy, there was no need for fancy when everyone sat on the same benches and ate at the same table. A practical chair, for Ailsa, who'd been sitting in a chair with a cracked leg for three months and hadn't complained because Ailsa didn't complain, she just endured, and Gareth had noticed and was building her a new one without being asked because that was what you did.

He was talking to the wood. Not loudly — Gareth never did anything loudly, he was a large man with a quiet voice, the kind of person who took up space gently. He was murmuring to the plank in his hands the way you'd murmur to a horse you were calming, and the wood was responding — not magically, not with circles, just with the grain shifting slightly under his hands, aligning, settling, becoming what it wanted to become. Gareth didn't have the gift, not really, not the way I did. He had something adjacent. Something that let him feel where the wood wanted to be cut, where the grain wanted to run, where a knot would strengthen or weaken a joint. He didn't cast circles. He just listened.

I stood at the edge of his workshop and watched him work for a moment. The chair was taking shape under his hands — legs tapered, seat curved to fit a small person's frame, back angled for comfort. Ailsa's chair. Made for her body. Made by someone who'd been watching her sit for years and knew exactly where she needed support.

He looked up. "Checking the north path?"

"Should be clear. The big oak by the creek dropped a limb in the rains, but I moved it last week."

"Was it heavy?"

"Grandmother moved it. I just asked."

He smiled. Not at the joke — there was no joke, it was just true — but at the way I said it, casual, like reporting the weather. He'd heard me say things like that my whole life. He never questioned them. He just nodded, the way the village nodded at anything Grandmother did, and went back to his chair.

"Grandmother likes your singing," he said, his eyes on the wood.

I thought he meant Maven. My grandmother. The one who taught me the songs. "She's been dead three years, Gareth."

"I know." He didn't look up. He planed the seat smooth, long strokes, the shavings curling at his feet like pale ribbons. "I said what I said."

I didn't know what he meant. I didn't ask. Gareth said things sometimes that landed like stones in deep water — you heard the splash, you saw the ripples, and then the water closed over it and you couldn't find the stone anymore. I'd learned to let his stones sink.

I checked the north path. It was clear. The oak limb was gone — moved, or grown over, or asked politely to relocate. The path opened through the forest the way paths in the Vale always opened: naturally, as if they'd always been there, as if the trees had simply decided to stand a little further apart in this one spot and no further explanation was needed.

---

Evening in the shared hall.

The stew was good. Senna's stew was always good, even when the turnips were getting soft, because Senna cooked the way she did everything — with the confidence of someone who had been right so many times that doubt had stopped visiting. The hall was full. The table was full. Bodies and warmth and the sound of a village feeding itself, which is a specific sound — not the clink of cutlery that the merchant described from cities, but the scrape of wooden bowls on wooden tables, the tearing of bread, the low murmur of conversation that didn't need to be loud because everyone was close enough to whisper and be heard.

Torv was in his chair. He ate slowly — not like Della, who savored, but like a man who was thinking while he chewed. His eyes moved over the hall the way they always moved — counting, watching, carrying. He saw everything. He said nothing. The elder's job was to notice, not to comment, and Torv had been noticing for twenty years and had gotten very good at not commenting.

Senna sat beside him, talking to Mira about the seed bank — which varieties were holding, which were thinning, whether they should ask the merchant for new stock or try to save their own. Mira worried about the seeds the way Pell worried about the stores, which was to say constantly and with the conviction that catastrophe was always one bad season away. Senna listened, nodded, made a decision, and Mira relaxed, because Senna's decisions were the kind that made other people's worry unnecessary.

Across the hall, Fern was telling a story to the younger kids — Rowan and Sage and Pax — about the time she'd climbed to the top of the tallest oak in the outer wood and seen all the way to the edge of the forest. The story was probably half true. The seeing-all-the-way-to-the-edge part was probably the half that wasn't true, because the Vale's canopy was too thick for that, but the climbing part was definitely true because Fern would climb anything that held still long enough and several things that didn't. Sage was listening with wide eyes and the particular expression of a seven-year-old who was scared and fascinated in equal measure. Rowan was listening with the expression of a ten-year-old who knew the story was exaggerated and didn't care. Pax was listening with the expression of a twelve-year-old who was already planning to climb the same tree tomorrow.

Gareth sat with Brenna, his wife, who was talking about the weaving she was doing — bark cloth for the village's summer needs, which was a process that took weeks and involved soaking and beating and stretching the fibers until they were soft enough to wear. Brenna made everything the village wore. She'd made my traveling clothes — the ones I didn't know I'd need yet, the ones she was making because Brenna always made the village's clothes and didn't need a reason beyond that. Good boots. A warm cloak. A sewing kit. "You'll need these more than you need magic," she'd said, and she was right, and I didn't know yet how right she was.

Lief, their son, sat beside his father in the quiet, steady way he always sat — watching, learning, absorbing. He was sixteen and already good with his hands, already shaping wood the way Gareth shaped it, with that same patient attention to what the material wanted. He didn't talk much. He didn't need to. He was doing the thing, and the thing spoke for him.

Ailsa was at the head of the table. She ate a little — just enough to keep Mira from worrying — and watched a lot. Her eyes moved over the hall the way Torv's moved, but differently. Torv watched for problems. Ailsa watched for stories. Every face at the table was a story she knew, every interaction was a chapter she'd read before, every silence was a pause she was memorizing. She caught my eye across the hall and held it for a moment. Then she said, clear enough that the people nearby heard:

"Your grandmother sang the way other people breathe."

The table didn't go silent — that wasn't Ailsa's way, she didn't make pronouncements that stopped rooms. She made observations that settled into rooms like dust, finding their way into the cracks and staying there. The conversation continued. Thorn was still arguing with Willa about the irrigation channels. Moss was still not talking. Dara was still talking for both of them. But Ailsa's words landed somewhere in the middle of all of it and stayed.

I didn't know what she meant. My grandmother sang. Everyone knew that. Maven sang when she worked, sang when she cooked, sang when she healed. She sang the way the village sang — not as performance, not as magic, just as... accompaniment. The melody that went with the doing. Ailsa was saying something more than that, something about the nature of the singing itself, something about it being not an action but a state. Singing the way other people breathe. Not choosing to sing. Just... singing. The way lungs choose to breathe. The way the heart chooses to beat.

I didn't understand. But the words stayed, the way Ailsa's words always stayed, finding the cracks and settling in.

After dinner, I walked home.

The village was quiet in the way it was quiet at night — not silent, because nothing in the Vale was ever silent, but settled, like a body going to sleep. The paths between the houses were dark except where the moon found gaps in the canopy and laid silver on the packed earth. The shared hall was dark behind me. The garden beds were dark ahead. The well caught the moonlight on its stone rim and held it, a circle of silver in the dark.

I passed Corwin's house and heard him arguing with a recipe through the wall. Hesta's voice, calm, translating: "the ratio is fine. you're tired. go to bed." Corwin's voice, clipped: "the ratio is never fine. the ratio is always a negotiation." Hesta: "then negotiate tomorrow." A pause. Then silence. He'd gone to bed.

I passed Moss and Dara's house and heard Sage's voice, small, asking for a story. Dara's voice, warm, already starting one. Moss's voice — not a voice, just a presence, a silence in the room that was somehow louder than the story because Moss's silences had weight.

I passed Torv and Senna's house and saw a light in the window. Torv, sitting in his chair, not sleeping. Senna, already in bed, her breathing slow and deep. Torv was holding the village down even at night, even when the village was asleep, even when nobody needed him to hold anything. It was a habit he couldn't break. Or a weight he wouldn't put down.

I reached my own door. Bran was asleep — I could hear his breathing through the wall, steady, heavy, the breathing of a man who worked hard and slept deep. Della was asleep beside him, lighter, the way she did everything — noticing even in her sleep, turning toward him when he shifted, adjusting. Fenn was out — hunting, probably, or sitting in the dark somewhere in the outer wood, watching the forest with the attention of someone who loved it. Cael was in his room, and I could see the faint glow of a candle under his door, which meant he was reading — one of the few books the village owned, passed down, pages soft with handling. He read the way he asked questions: hungrily, like the words might run out.

I stood outside for a moment. The air was cool. The canopy above was a dark ceiling, barely visible, and the shapes of the great trunks rose around me like pillars in a cathedral that nobody had built because it had built itself. Somewhere in the dark, a nightbird called — not a song, just a check-in, a sound that said *I'm here, are you there,* and the forest answered with silence, which was its way of saying *yes.*

The path to my door was clear. The moonlight found it. The earth beneath my feet was warm.

I went inside. I put my hand on the hearthstone. Warm. Always warm.

I closed my eyes.

The village slept. The forest held it.
