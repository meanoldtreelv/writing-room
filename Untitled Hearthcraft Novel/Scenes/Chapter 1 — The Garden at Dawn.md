# Chapter 1 — The Garden at Dawn

The soil was warm under my fingers when I pressed my palm flat. Warmer than it should have been — the dawn was still new, the canopy hadn't finished sorting the light into its morning colors, and the air coming off the Vale had that thin bite it always carried before the sun found the gaps between the leaves. But the soil was warm. It was always warm. I didn't think about it.

I traced the groove around the first tomato bed with my thumb, following the curve of the inner ring where I'd drawn it yesterday with a stick from the woodpile. One circle, four symbols — the same pattern my grandmother taught me before I was old enough to hold a spoon properly. The groove was deeper than I'd left it. The earth had shifted overnight, settling the circle tighter, pulling the edges closed like skin over a wound that was healing faster than it should. The tomato plants inside the ring were green and firm, leaves broad, stems thick enough to hold fruit that hadn't come in yet. Outside the ring, the voles had been testing the edges again — little divots in the soft earth where they'd poked their noses in and thought better of it. They always thought better of it. The circles bit back. Not hard. Just enough.

I moved to the herb bed. Thyme was spreading past its border again, creeping over the packed earth toward the sage in that patient way it had, like it was testing whether anyone was paying attention. I pulled it back with my fingers, not roughly — thyme didn't respond well to rough — and tucked the runners under the main plant where they'd root and thicken. The sage needed cutting back. It was shading out the parsley on the north side, and the parsley was getting leggy, reaching for light that wasn't there. I'd cut it after breakfast. The cuttings would go to Dara for drying. Nothing in the village got wasted. Waste was what happened when you stopped paying attention.

The path through the village was the same as every morning. The well first — I drew a cup of water and drank it standing, one hand on the stone rim that was worn smooth by a thousand hands before mine. The water tasted like the forest: mineral, green, old. Like drinking from a river that had been filtering through roots for longer than anyone could count. The well was the center of everything. Not the shared hall, not Torv's house, not the garden beds. The well. You drew water, you heard the news, you knew where everyone was and what they were doing and whether Old Grumpy — that was what Thorn called the tomato bed nearest the eastern root — was cooperating today. The well was where the village breathed.

Senna was in the shared hall when I passed, arguing with a pot that had been disagreeable since before I was born. The pot was too big for the fire and too small for the batch and the handle was loose and every few months someone would say "we should fix the handle" and then nobody would and Senna would keep arguing with it. She saw me through the doorway and jerked her chin toward the bread cooling on the table. "Take some. Don't be polite about it." I wasn't. The bread was good — Senna's bread was always good, even when the pot was disagreeable — and I ate it walking, the crust cracking between my teeth, the inside soft and warm.

Torv was in his usual chair on the hall's front step, not eating yet, just sitting with his hands on his knees like he was holding himself down. He was the only one in the village who sat like that — planted, heavy, rooted. Everyone else moved. Torv stayed. He'd been elder since his father died twenty years ago, and the role wasn't elected, it was accepted, the way you accept gravity or winter. Someone had to talk to the merchant. Someone had to keep the calendar. Someone had to sit on the step and hold the village down. Torv did it because nobody else wanted to, and he did it well because he'd been doing it so long he'd forgotten how to do anything else.

He nodded at me as I passed. I nodded back. We didn't need to say anything. We'd been nodding at each other every morning for years. His eyes were the same as always — steady, watching, carrying something heavy that he never put down. I was eighteen and I'd been seeing that look my whole life and I still didn't know what it weighed. I didn't ask. In the village, you didn't ask about weight. You just noticed who was carrying it and made sure they ate.

I found Thorn at the garden beds, already arguing with the soil. He was kneeling in the fallow bed — the one he called "the Diplomat" because it was the only bed that cooperated without complaining — turning earth with his hands. Not a hoe. His hands. Thorn said you could feel what the soil needed through your palms, the way Corwin said he could feel what a wound needed through his fingertips. Thorn didn't have the gift. Neither did Corwin, really — Corwin could feel things but couldn't fix them, which had made him the best herb doctor in the village and the most frustrated man in it. Thorn just had good hands.

"Today's the north beds," he said without looking up. "The ones by the big root. Carrots and the second planting of greens."

I looked at the beds. The soil was dark and loose — Thorn had been turning it for a week, working in the compost from the shared heap. It was ready. "I'll circle them after breakfast."

"Circle them now. I want to see if the carrots will take before noon."

"They'll take."

"They'd better. I named that bed 'the Stubborn' and I don't want it living up to it."

I knelt beside him and drew the circle. My finger in the dirt, one ring, four symbols. The same shape I'd drawn a thousand times. The same shape my grandmother drew ten thousand times before me. The soil was warm under my finger — always warm — and I poured intent into the groove as I traced it: grow, hold, protect. The circle settled. The earth shifted slightly, tightening around the ring, sealing it. I could feel it hum — not with my ears, but with my palm where it rested on the ground. A low vibration, slow, deep, like the earth was breathing.

Thorn watched. He always watched. He didn't understand what I did — nobody in the village understood it the way an outsider might, because there was nothing to understand, it was just tending — but he understood results. The carrots would come in strong. The greens would hold against the shade. The bed would do what it was supposed to do.

"Good enough," he said, and went back to the Diplomat.

I went to breakfast.

---

The shared hall was full by the time I got there, which meant I was late, which meant Fern had already eaten and gone, which meant she'd be waiting for me somewhere between the hall and the outer wood, probably up a tree, definitely muddy. The hall smelled like bread and broth and the particular warmth that comes from a room full of people who've been eating together so long they've run out of secrets. Bodies around the long table. Willa at the end, silent, eating with the focused efficiency of someone who had a day of hard labor ahead and no time for conversation. Beside her, Ash — their son, broad-shouldered, quiet, eating with his eyes on his plate and his ears on Fenn, who was telling a hunting story that was probably only half true. Fenn's stories were always half true. The half that was true was the part where he nearly died. The half that wasn't was the part where he was brave about it.

Corwin was at the herb table in the corner — not the main table, the side table where he spread out his dried plants and his pestle and his notes. He ate the way he worked: precisely, methodically, without looking up. Hesta sat beside him, calm, the bridge between Corwin and everyone else. When Corwin was irritable — which was always — Hesta translated. "He's not angry, he'd worried," she'd say. "Same face, different reason." Pax, their son, twelve years old and full of questions, was sitting next to Corwin and asking him something about poultice ratios that Corwin was answering in the clipped, precise way he answered everything, and Pax was nodding like he understood, which he didn't, but he would eventually, because Pax was the kind of kid who asked until he got it.

Moss appeared the way Moss always appeared — silently, without transition, like he'd been standing in the corner waiting for someone to notice and then gave up and sat down. He took his food. He ate. He didn't speak. The village joked that Moss used his yearly word budget on the merchant, and the joke was funny because it was close to true. Moss spent days in the forest, foraging, mushroom-keeping, mapping the deep paths that only he knew. He came back with baskets of things that shouldn't have existed in a forest this dark — luminous fungi, roots that tasted like honey, mushrooms so rare that the merchant's eyes went wide when he saw them. Moss pointed at things. Moss carried things. Moss was the quietest essential person in the village and everyone knew it.

Dara followed him in — warm, chatty, compensating. She sat next to Moss and immediately started talking to Willa about the drying racks, something about the airflow on the east side being wrong, and Willa grunted, which was Willa's way of saying "I'll fix it after lunch." Rowan and Sage, Moss and Dara's kids, were at the far end of the table — Rowan ten, Sage seven, both too young for much but old enough to eat in the hall and listen to the stories. Sage was scared of everything, which the village found endearing rather than concerning. Pax was his best friend, which helped, because Pax was scared of nothing and Sage could borrow his courage when he needed it.

Pell was not at breakfast. Pell was in the granary. Pell was always in the granary. He'd eat later, standing up, while counting things. Mira was there in his place — gentle, worried, the village's conscience. She saw me sit down and pushed a second bowl of broth toward me without being asked. "You're too thin," she said. "I'm fine." "You're too thin and you're too polite about it. Eat." I ate.

Ailsa was at the head of the table. She was always at the head of the table, not because she was the elder — Torv was the elder — but because she was the oldest and the table had learned to arrange itself around her the way a river arranges itself around a stone. She was tiny, fierce, immovable. Seventy years old and she looked like she'd been seventy for decades, like she'd decided to be seventy and the years had agreed. She was watching the hall the way she always watched — not eating, not talking, just holding the room in her eyes. She knew every story. Every song. Every name of every person who'd ever sat at this table. She'd taught us the village's songs when we were children — not the magical ones, the *other* ones. Lullabies and work songs and the planting hymn that everyone sang in spring without knowing why it mattered. Ailsa knew why it mattered. She didn't say.

I finished the broth. I stood.

"Where are you going?" Mira asked.

"The outer wood. Fern's waiting."

"Fern's been waiting since dawn. She can wait another minute. Sit." Mira's hand on my wrist — gentle, but firm enough that I sat. She looked at me for a moment, the way she sometimes looked at me, like she was counting something. Then she let go. "Go. But eat more when you get back."

I went.

---

Fern was up a tree. Of course she was.

She was fifteen feet up in one of the big oaks at the edge of the outer wood, wedged in a crook where the trunk split into three, her legs dangling, her hair full of bark. She saw me coming and grinned the grin that meant she'd been waiting long enough to be impatient about it but not long enough to be angry.

"You're late."

"I was working."

"You're always working." She dropped — fifteen feet, casual, landing in a crouch that sent leaves scattering. She was wiry and quick and loud and everything I wasn't. She straightened, brushed bark off her shirt, and looked at me with the particular expression she reserved for moments when she was about to suggest something I'd say no to.

"Race you to the creek."

"I just got here."

"Exactly. You need to warm up."

"I'm warm."

"You're *stiff.* You've been kneeling in the dirt since dawn. Come on. One race. If you win, I'll carry your herbs back."

"I don't need you to carry my herbs."

"If I win, you make me soup tonight."

I looked at her. She was already bouncing on her toes. Her boots were muddy. Her knees were muddy. There was a leaf in her hair that she hadn't noticed and that I wasn't going to tell her about because it was funny.

"Fine," I said.

She ran before I finished the word.

I followed. Not because I thought I'd win — Fern was faster, Fern was always faster, Fern had been faster since we were six and she'd beaten me to the well and celebrated by falling in it — but because running with Fern was the closest thing I had to magic that wasn't magic. The forest opened around us. The outer wood was normal trees, normal light, normal paths — nothing like the Vale, where the canopy closed overhead and the air tasted green. Out here the sky showed through in patches and the light was honest and the ground was flat enough to run on if you knew where to put your feet. Fern knew. I knew. We'd been running these paths since we were children.

She beat me to the creek by a body length. She spun, triumphant, arms up, mud on her face.

"Soup," she said.

"Tonight," I said.

"And bread."

"Senna makes the bread."

"Senna will make the bread if you ask. You're her favorite."

"I'm not her favorite."

"You're everyone's favorite and you don't even know it." She dropped onto the bank, legs in the water, and looked at me with the sudden seriousness that sometimes caught me off guard because Fern wasn't supposed to be serious. "Are you okay? You've been quiet."

"I'm always quiet."

"You're quiet-quiet. Not Brin-quiet. There's a difference."

I sat beside her. The creek was cold — snowmelt from the mountains, filtered through a mile of forest before it reached us. The water was clear enough to see the stones on the bottom, each one worn smooth, each one exactly where the current had decided it should be.

"I'm fine," I said.

Fern looked at me for a long moment. Then she splashed me.

"Good," she said. "Now come on. If we're late for lunch, Senna will make us eat with the toddlers."

We walked back. The forest was quiet around us — not silent, because nothing in the Vale was ever silent, but settled. The canopy filtered the afternoon light into something green and gold and slow. Somewhere above, a bird was arguing with itself. Below, the roots hummed. Or the soil hummed. Or the well hummed. It was all the same hum and we didn't have a word for it because you don't name the thing that holds you up.

---

I made dinner that night.

Not because it was my turn — there was no turn, the village cooked the way it breathed, whoever was in the hall when the pot needed filling filled it — but because I wanted to. The root vegetables from the morning's harvest. Herbs from the garden — the thyme I'd tucked back, a handful of sage, parsley from the north side that was getting leggy but still tasted right. Broth from the bones Senna had been saving. The fire was already going when I got there, the shared hearth built between the three great trunks that held the hall's roof, the flames steady in that way village fires were steady — not because anyone enchanted them, but because the wood was right and the hearth was old and the fire knew where it belonged.

I put the pot on. I circled it with the stirring spoon — one ring, four symbols, traced in the steam. Not because I had to. Not because the soup needed magic. Because that was what you did when you cooked in the village. My grandmother did it. Her grandmother did it. You stirred, you circled, you hummed, and the food came out better. Not dramatically better. Not glow-in-the-dark better. Just... *more.* More filling. More warming. More like what your body needed and less like what you thought you wanted.

I hummed. Not a tune I chose. The one that was there. The melody was old — my grandmother sang it, and her grandmother sang it, and back and back, a chain of women standing at hearths, stirring pots, humming a song that nobody remembered learning because you didn't learn it, you just knew it the way you knew your own name. The melody was simple. It went up, it went down, it curled around the words that weren't really words, just shapes that the song needed to hold itself together. It sounded like the forest. It sounded like the soil. It sounded like the well at dawn.

The fire burned steadier. The broth thickened. The herbs released their scent in a wave — thyme first, then sage, then parsley, each one layering over the last until the whole hall smelled like the garden in summer. The steam rose in a spiral. Not a casual spiral, not the way steam normally rises, all wispy and directionless. This steam had a shape. It curled up from the pot and turned once, twice, three times, and then settled into a column that went straight up and disappeared into the canopy above.

Outside the kitchen window, the branches leaned toward the glass. Just slightly. Like someone tilting their head to listen. I couldn't see it. I was stirring. The branches held that angle for a long moment — long enough that if anyone had been watching, they would have noticed. Then they settled back, slowly, like they'd decided the song was done.

The village ate.

Bran ate without comment. He always ate without comment. My father was a man of actions, not words — he'd taught me to check the garden, to read the soil, to know which mushrooms were safe, and he'd done it all by doing it beside me and letting me copy. He ate the soup the way he ate everything: steadily, thoroughly, with the focused attention of a man who considered meals a form of refueling. But tonight he finished the bowl and sat back and closed his eyes for a moment. Just a moment. When he opened them, he looked at me, and his face did something I'd only seen a few times — a softening, a warmth, a thing he'd never say out loud because Bran didn't say things out loud. He said "good soup" and went back to eating. It was enough.

Della ate slowly. My mother always ate slowly, savoring, not because she was delicate but because she was paying attention. She tasted things. She noticed things. She was the one who'd known I had the gift before anyone else — had noticed the garden growing faster around my favorite sitting spot, the fire burning steadier when I hummed. She never said anything about it. She just watched, the way she watched everything, with that sharp, warm attention that saw everything and judged nothing. She finished her bowl and looked at me and said "your grandmother would have liked this one" and went back to eating. I didn't know what she meant. I didn't ask.

Fenn ate fast. My oldest brother ate the way he did everything — quickly, efficiently, as if the world might change while he was chewing and he wanted to be ready. He reached for a second bowl and I pushed it toward him without being asked. He grunted his thanks. His forearm had a new scratch from hunting — a branch that shouldn't have been there, or a bramble that fought back. I'd heal it after dinner. He knew I would. He didn't ask. In the village, you didn't ask for healing. You just sat near me long enough and I noticed and fixed it. That was how it worked.

Cael ate and watched me. My second brother was always watching — not suspicious, not jealous, just *curious.* Cael wanted to know *why.* Why the garden grew. Why the circles worked. Why Grandmother opened paths. The village found this exhausting. I found it interesting, and a little worrying, because Cael was the kind of person who would eventually leave. Everyone knew it. He talked about the road, about the merchant's stories, about the world beyond the forest. He wasn't unhappy. He was hungry. There was a difference, and Cael was the only one who seemed to feel it.

"When you hum," he said, quietly, across the table, while Fenn was telling Ash about the brambles, "what do you hear?"

"Nothing," I said.

He looked at me. He didn't believe me. He never believed me when I said that. He was probably right not to.

After dinner, the hall emptied slowly. People said goodnight in the way village people said goodnight — not with words, but with touches. A hand on a shoulder. A squeeze of an arm. Willa squeezed my shoulder on the way out, hard, the way she always did, and I'd never asked why because the squeeze was the communication and the why didn't matter. Moss left without saying anything. Dara left while saying everything. Ailsa was the last to go, or maybe she didn't go, maybe she just became part of the hall the way the hearthstones were part of the hall, permanent, immovable, watching.

I sat by the fire after they left. The hall was empty. The embers were dying. I traced small circles in the ash with my fingertip — not casting, not intending, just moving my hand the way it wanted to move. The ash settled into patterns. Not from my breath, not from the draft. It just... settled. Slowly. Like it was arranging itself into something. I watched it for a while. I didn't know what it meant. I didn't need to.

The fire hummed. The forest hummed. The soil hummed. It was all the same hum, and I was part of it, and the village was part of it, and the trees above us were part of it, and somewhere in the deep roots beneath the hall, beneath the garden, beneath the well, something old and alive and patient was listening to a girl trace circles in the ash and was glad.

I put my hand on the hearthstone. Warm. Always warm.

I closed my eyes.

The village slept. The forest held it.
