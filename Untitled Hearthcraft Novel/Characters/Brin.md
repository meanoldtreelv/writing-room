# Brin

## Root Logic
**"If I can fix it, I should."**
Not heroism — obligation. The village couldn't afford not to use every gift it had. Brin's magic was never separate from her daily life. It was the same impulse that made her check the garden at dawn, mend a fence before it fell, cook a meal when someone looked thin. **This is also why she leaves.** When Torv tells her the truth about Maven and the night of screaming, she sees the problem: if she stays, Grandmother protects her, the empire notices, soldiers come, the forest burns. The only variable she can control is her own presence. So she goes. She chooses the road so the forest doesn't have to choose violence.

## Core Identity
- **Age:** 17-18 (of age for crown assessment)
- **Origin:** Village called "Rootwell" in imperial records — but the village has no name for itself. It's just home. The name only exists on old maps and in pre-imperial texts about willed-casting traditions. Maren Selt recognizes it. Brin has never heard it.
- **Family:** Working family — parents without the gift, not afraid of it. Grandmother (deceased) had the gift and taught Brin the shapes and the songs before she died. The songs come from the forest — passed down through generations of women who listened.
- **Magic Style:** Willed casting. **Single inner circle only** — no outer binding rings. Drawn with whatever's available — dirt, blood, soup, chalk, ash. No formal verbal incantation — pure intent. For complex magic, she hums half-remembered songs her grandmother taught her (lullabies, work songs, planting hymns — not chants, melodies). She doesn't know she's casting. It's just "tending." To academy eyes, her single-ring circles look *unfinished* — like someone who gave up halfway through.

## Flaw
**Usefulness as worth.** If Brin can't help, she's nothing. She's terrible at receiving help, asking for things, or resting. She equates her value with her output. This makes her generous to a fault and absolutely exhausting to herself.

## Arc
Movement 0: She doesn't know she's special. Magic is just work.
Movement I: She arrives at the academy and discovers her "work" is invisible to the system. Scores zeros.
Movement II: She starts questioning — not whether she's weak, but whether the system is *wrong.*
Movement III: She discovers the old texts. Learns what she does has a name and a history. Gets better — not flashier, more precise.
Movement IV: She refuses the academy's frame. Doesn't want to reform the institution — wants to go home with what she's learned.
Movement V: Returns to the village. Builds something new with old knowledge.

## Key Relationships
- **Caius:** Philosophical collision. He's everything she's not — loud, privileged, certain. She's everything he can't understand — quiet, useful, certain in a completely different way. Romance develops through friction and soup.
- **Lira:** Political translator and romantic wildcard. Lira sees what Brin can't — the system's gears. Brin gives Lira something she's never had — someone who values her for what she *does,* not whose blood she carries.
- **Maren Selt:** The professor who sees. Quiet ally. Gives Brin access, context, and theory — but never tells her what to do.

## Signature Moments
- **The Cook Scene (Movement 0):** Makes a meal, circles the pot with the stirring spoon. The family eats, sleeps well, heals faster. The most intimate magic in the book.
- **The Unfinished Circle (Movement I):** Draws one ring in class. Students laugh. Professor frowns. She hums a fragment of grandmother's song. The circle glows — not bright, warm. Like hearthlight. The laughter stops.
- **The Blister Scene (Movement I):** Heals a fellow student's blister with a thumb and intent. No words. The student stares. "That's... that's not a spell." "It's just a blister."
- **The Grandmother's Library (Movement III):** Maren shows her a pre-imperial fragment with single-circle notation and musical annotation. Brin hums. The notation matches — she's been singing this song her whole life without knowing it was ever written down.
- **The Humming Healer (Movement IV):** Works a crisis zone with circles drawn in mud, blood, ash. Humming while she works. But the work is *hard* — the soil is dead, forced into farmland by the empire, no root network, no voice in the earth. She's singing in an empty room for the first time. She sees confederation aid workers doing the same thing without magic. She recognizes it. *They listened to the river. The empire shouted at it.*

## Thematic Function
Brin IS the thesis. She doesn't argue for hearthcraft — she embodies it. Every time she draws a circle in the dirt, she's proving that magic doesn't need to be loud to be real.

## Name Notes
- "Brin" — hard 'b', clipped, stops short. Old root for "hill" or "from the high place." Sounds like someone who doesn't waste syllables.
- Alternate considered: Senna (healing plant — thematic but slightly exotic), Moss (too soft), Clay (reads masculine).
