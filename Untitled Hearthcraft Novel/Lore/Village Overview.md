# Village Overview вҖ” Rootwell

## The Name
**The village has no name for itself.** It's just home. The name "Rootwell" exists only in imperial records and pre-imperial texts. Maren Selt recognizes it. Brin has never heard it. The village doesn't need a name вҖ” names are how the empire makes things legible.

## Demographics
- **9 families**, approximately **45-55 people**
- Everyone knows everyone. Everyone owes everyone.
- Communal subsistence вҖ” shared stores, shared tasks, no private wealth
- Families have been here for generations beyond counting
- Magic is a communal resource вҖ” "tending," not "casting"

## The Forest вҖ” Grandmother
The village lives inside the ancient old-growth forest. The trees are redwood-scale вҖ” bigger around than a carriage. The canopy is so high you can't see it.

**The forest is alive.** The village calls her Grandmother. She is a sentient entity вҖ” not a character who speaks, but a presence that acts. Paths open for lost children. Branches shift. The soil is warm. Children are deposited home after dark.

**THIS IS NEVER STATED IN THE TEXT.** The reader discovers it through context.

### What Grandmother Does (shown, never explained)
- Opens paths for lost children (a child says "grandma opened a path for me" вҖ” the mother nods like it's weather)
- Deposits naughty children home after dark
- Keeps the soil warm for the garden circles
- Leans branches toward the kitchen window when someone hums
- The songs are conversation вҖ” when Brin hums, Grandmother listens

### What Grandmother Did
**Fifty years ago,** when the assessors came and Maven passed their test, she didn't want to go. Grandmother got rid of those who would try to take her grandchildren against their will. **It was a night of screaming.** Torv will never forget.

The details are never spoken of. The village doesn't discuss it. But everyone knows:
- The assessors came with a mandate
- Maven refused
- The forest refused with her
- Nobody survived to report back
- The empire sent no one for fifty years

This is not why Brin leaves. **Brin chooses to leave.** The night after her test, Torv tells her the truth. She sits with it. Then she stands. "I'll go." She chooses the road so the forest doesn't have to choose violence. Her root logic: *if I can fix it, I should.* She fixes this by removing herself.

### Grandmother's Response to Brin's Departure
- It gets cold despite summer вҖ” not winter-cold, *sad*-cold
- The villagers bring in extra wood. "Grandmother's cold today."
- The paths still open for other children вҖ” Grandmother doesn't abandon the village
- But it takes longer. A father doesn't sound sure.
- Brin walks south and the trees are still. No paths. No branches swaying. Just silence.

## Trade and Economy
**Annual merchant visit** вҖ” barter, not currency. The village trades:
- Dried mushrooms (rare, valuable вҖ” massive fungal networks)
- Herbal medicines, healing salves
- Forest honey (from huge canopy hives)
- Carved woodwork

For:
- Metal tools (no smelting)
- Salt (no deposits)
- Cloth/fabric (can't grow fiber in shade)
- Seed stock
- News (most valuable import)

## Crops
- Shade-adapted: root vegetables, mushrooms, leafy greens, herbs
- No grain (too little light) вҖ” grain from merchant or Outer Wood clearings
- Light coaxed downward via hearthcraft вҖ” single circles in the soil
- It's always a good growing season. (Grandmother makes sure of it.)

## Winters
Brutal. The village survives through hearthcraft: sustaining charms on stores, warming circles in homes, preservation magic on food. Grandmother holds the village through winter вҖ” roots beneath houses radiate warmth, stores don't spoil. This isn't optional. It's survival.

## Village Dialogue Patterns
Things characters say without explanation вҖ” the reader pieces them together:
- "grandma opened a path for me"
- "Grandmother brought you home?"
- "that's just Grandmother"
- "Grandmother's cold today" (when it's unseasonably chilly)
- "ask Grandmother" (when someone can't find something)
- "Grandmother knows" (when something works out)
- "don't worry about it" (when outsiders see something strange)
