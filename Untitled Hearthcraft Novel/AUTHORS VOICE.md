# Author's Voice Guide

## The Foundation

### Robert Jordan (The Primary Influence)
What we take from Jordan:
- **Accumulated detail.** The world is built through texture — what the room smells like, how the light falls, which stones are worn smooth by hands. Every object has a history. Every space has a mood.
- **Sensory immersion.** The reader should feel the dirt under Brin's fingers, hear the chanting echo off stone walls, taste the soup. We don't say "it was cold." We say what cold does to fingers, to breath, to the sound a door makes when the wood has contracted.
- **Internal character life.** Characters think. They notice things. They're wrong about what they notice. Their observations reveal who they are without the narrator explaining it.
- **Repetition as rhythm.** Character tics, recurring phrases, familiar rituals. The village's morning routine should feel like a song the reader learns the melody of. When the routine breaks, the reader feels it.
- **World-building through implication.** We don't explain the empire's age. We show a stone worn smooth by a thousand hands. We don't explain the doctrine's rigidity. We show a student who can't remember the words and freezes.
- **Prose you disappear into.** The reader should lose track of time. The sentences should flow like water — not choppy, not jarring, not interrupted by tonal shifts. You sink in. You stay under. The world carries you.

### Pratchett (Secondary Influence — Characterization Only)
What we take from Pratchett:
- **Characters being themselves.** Moss speaks once a year. Thorn names his garden beds. Ailsa sings for three days at a grave. These moments are funny, warm, and true — not because the narrator makes a joke, but because the *character* is exactly who they are and the reader recognizes it.
- **The human beneath the epic.** Pratchett never lets the scale overwhelm the person. The empire is vast. The flood is devastating. And Pell is still counting seeds because that's what Pell *does.*
- **Dialogue as character.** Every character sounds different. Not because of accent or dialect — because of *what they pay attention to.* Bran says practical things. Cael asks questions. Ailsa speaks in stories. Fern is loud. Moss is silent until he isn't.

What we do NOT take from Pratchett:
- **Tonal cuts.** No sharp one-liners that crystallize the theme. No sentences that pull the reader up to look at the joke or the insight. The meaning should flow *through* the prose, not land *on top of* it.
- **Winking narration.** No narrator asides, no footnotes, no "and this is the funny part" framing. The humor is in the texture, not in the delivery.
- **Irony as structure.** No scenes built around a punchline. Scenes are built around immersion. The irony is in what the character doesn't notice, not in what the narrator points out.

## The Voice — Immersive Prose

### The Core Principle
**The reader should get lost.** Not in confusion — in *presence.* The prose should be a river that carries them. Every sentence leads to the next. Every detail adds to the current. When the scene changes — village to academy, calm to crisis — the reader should feel the *water* change, not the narrator's tone.

### POV Discipline
- **First person (Brin's POV) for the main narrative.** Everything is filtered through her experience. She doesn't know Grandmother is alive. She doesn't know her magic is unusual. She doesn't know the empire is wrong. The reader discovers these things through the gap between what Brin takes for granted and what the reader recognizes as extraordinary.
- **Third person limited for other POVs** (if we use them). Caius's chapters see the world through noble eyes — status, hierarchy, performance. Lira's chapters see the world through political eyes — who has power, who's pretending, who's vulnerable.
- **The narrator never explains magic.** The narrator never explains Grandmother. The narrator never explains the empire's doctrine. Characters experience these things. The reader assembles the meaning.

### Description Rules

**Rule 1: Describe what the POV character would notice.**
Brin notices soil temperature, plant health, the sound of water, how someone's breathing. She doesn't notice architecture, fashion, or political symbolism. Caius notices all of those things. Lira notices who's watching.

**Rule 2: Every description serves double duty.**
The stone is worn smooth (world-building: this place is old) AND it's warm under Brin's fingers (plot: Grandmother is listening). The academy's lights are bright (world-building: no shadows, no secrets) AND Brin squints (character: she's used to dappled green light).

**Rule 3: Sensory details before abstractions.**
Don't say "the village was communal." Show Della sending someone home with soup. Show Thorn arguing with the soil. Show the shared hall full of bodies at dinner. The reader *concludes* it's communal. We never say it.

**Rule 4: The accumulation.**
Build scenes through layered detail. The departure scene isn't "Brin left." It's: the soil was warm. the hearth was circled. the stores were full. the air got cold. Fern gave her a stone. Bran said "you write." the trees were still. Each detail is a brick. The wall builds itself. The reader doesn't notice the wall until it's done — and then they can't look away from it.

**Rule 5: Let the detail carry the meaning.**
Don't cut to the insight. Don't crystallize. Let the accumulated detail *become* the meaning. The reader who has been watching Brin circle the garden, say goodbye to the well, feel the cold, watch the trees go still — that reader *knows* what it means. They don't need a sentence to tell them. The silence after the last detail is more powerful than any line we could write.

### Prose Style

**Flow over fragmentation.**
Sentences should connect. Paragraphs should flow. The reader should feel like they're being carried downstream, not stepping stone to stone. Use compound sentences, em dashes, semicolons — whatever keeps the current moving.

**Rhythm over variety.**
Repeat sentence structures when the scene calls for it. A series of short sentences creates urgency. A long, flowing sentence creates calm. The village chapters should breathe long. The crisis chapters should breathe short. But within each rhythm, *stay in it.* Don't break the pattern for variety's sake.

**The sentence that earns its length.**
Jordan writes long sentences. So can we. But every long sentence must earn its length through *specificity.* Not "the forest was old and big and dark and mysterious." The forest was old in the way that made you feel like a guest in someone else's house — the kind of house where the floorboards remembered everyone who'd walked on them and the walls had opinions about where you put your hands.

**The sentence that earns its brevity.**
Not every sentence needs to be long. "She didn't look back." Three words. After a page of accumulated detail, three words can hold more weight than a paragraph. But this is earned by what came before — it's the silence after the music, not a jump cut.

### Dialogue Rules

**Rule 1: Characters speak in their own register.**
- Brin: practical, short sentences, understated. She says "it's just a blister" when she's performed magic that shouldn't be possible.
- Caius: precise, formal, self-conscious. He speaks like someone who was taught that words matter because they reflect on the house.
- Lira: careful, strategic, warm when she forgets to be strategic. She asks questions more than she makes statements.
- Maren: quiet, layered, says less than she knows. She asks Brin to show her things rather than explaining them.
- The village: practical, communal, uses "Grandmother" the way other people use "the weather."
- Torv: heavy, deliberate, each word costs him something.

**Rule 2: What characters DON'T say matters more.**
Brin doesn't say goodbye to Grandmother because the village has no word for goodbye. Della doesn't say "I'm terrified" — she braids Brin's hair. Bran doesn't say "I love you" — he says "you write when you can." The silence between words is where the meaning lives.

**Rule 3: Dialogue reveals worldview.**
When a noble student says "you forgot the outer rings," they're revealing the entire academy's doctrine in a single sentence. When Brin says "what outer rings?" she's revealing the entire village's understanding. The collision of worldviews happens in conversation, not in narration.

**Rule 4: Humor flows from truth, not from delivery.**
Moss's word budget is funny because it's *true.* Thorn's named beds are funny because Thorn is *exactly* that kind of person. The humor is in the character, not in the narrator's framing. We show Moss being quiet. We show Thorn naming beds. The reader smiles because they *know* these people. We don't wink at the reader. We don't signal "this is the funny part." We just show it and trust.

### Pacing

**The long breath.**
Movement 0 (the village) should feel slow, warm, immersive. The reader lives here. Every detail is a brick in a wall they don't realize they're building until it's done. Don't rush. Don't cut. Let the village breathe.

**The gradual shift.**
When Brin leaves the village, the prose doesn't suddenly change. It *thins.* The sentences get slightly shorter. The details get slightly less warm. The sensory anchors shift from soil and fire and growing things to stone and light and echoing halls. The reader feels the change because the *texture* changes, not because the tone shifts.

**The crisis acceleration.**
When the flood hits, the prose tightens. Not dramatically — the sentences don't suddenly become fragments. But the details get sharper, more immediate, more physical. Mud. Water. Wounds. The river. The prose accelerates the way a river accelerates before a falls — you don't notice until you're already going fast.

**Never break the surface.**
No scene should end on a "gotcha." No paragraph should land on a punchline. The reader should finish a chapter and feel like they've been *somewhere,* not like they've been *told* something. The immersion is the point. The immersion is always the point.

### Thematic Delivery

**Never state the theme.** The theme is: magic measured by what it feeds, not what it impresses. We never say this. We show Brin feeding people. We show the academy impressing people. The reader draws the conclusion.

**Let the motif do the work.** Brin draws circles. The academy draws circles. Same shape, different meaning. We never explain the difference. We show Brin's circle glow warm. We show the academy's circle glow bright. The reader sees the difference. And the reader who's been immersed in the prose for 200 pages doesn't need us to point it out — they *feel* it.

**The meaning is in the silence.** After all the accumulated detail, sometimes the most powerful thing is what we *don't* write. Brin walks south and the trees are still. We don't say "Grandmother was grieving." We show the cold. We show the silence. We show the path that didn't open. The reader who's been living in this world knows what it means. Trust them.

## Scene Construction Template

Every scene should have:

1. **A sensory anchor** — one vivid detail the reader can hold. The soil is warm. The chanting echoes. The stone is worn smooth.
2. **A character action** — something the POV character does that reveals who they are. Brin hums. Caius adjusts his cuffs. Lira asks a question.
3. **A gap** — something the POV character doesn't notice or understand that the reader does. Brin doesn't notice the branches leaning. Caius doesn't notice the waste. Lira doesn't notice her own loneliness.
4. **Accumulated texture** — enough detail that the reader feels *present.* Not summarized. Not sketched. *There.*
5. **A natural ending** — the scene ends when the moment is complete, not when the narrator decides to make a point. The last detail should be a sensory anchor, a character action, or a silence. Never a thesis statement.

---

## What We Avoid

- **Exposition dumps.** If the reader needs to know something, a character experiences it. If no character experiences it, the reader doesn't need to know it yet.
- **Named emotions.** Don't say "Brin was sad." Show what Brin does when she's sad. She circles the garden. She hums. She doesn't look back.
- **Tonal cuts.** No single sentences that crystallize the theme. No lines that pull the reader up to look at the insight. The meaning is in the current, not in the splash.
- **Winking narration.** No narrator asides. No "and isn't that something." No fourth wall. The narrator is invisible. The world is everything.
- **Punchline structures.** No scenes built around a reveal. Scenes are built around immersion. The reveal is in what the reader assembles, not in what the narrator delivers.
- **Villain speeches.** The dean doesn't monologue about why spectacle matters. He *demonstrates* it. The reader watches and understands.
- **Convenient explanations.** Maren doesn't explain the old magic to Brin in one scene. She opens a door. Brin walks through it. The understanding comes over chapters.
- **Purple prose.** Jordan's detail is *specific,* not ornate. We aim for specificity. Every word earns its place. Long sentences earn their length through detail, not decoration.

## Sample Passages (To Calibrate Voice)

### Village (Immersion — Jordan-style)
> Brin checked the garden at dawn. The tomato circles were holding — soil warm under her fingers when she pressed her palm flat, leaves green and firm, nothing nibbled along the edges where the voles usually tested. She traced a finger along the groove she'd made yesterday, following the curve of the inner ring. It was deeper than she'd left it. The earth shifted overnight, settling the circle tighter, pulling the groove closed like skin over a healing cut. She didn't notice. She moved on to the herb beds, where the thyme was spreading past its border and the sage needed cutting back before it shaded out the parsley.
>
> The morning was quiet in the way mornings were quiet in the Vale — not silent, because nothing in the forest was ever silent, but settled. The canopy filtered the dawn into something green and gold and slow, and the air tasted like wet wood and old rain. Somewhere above, a bird was arguing with itself. Below, the roots hummed. Or the soil hummed. Or the well hummed. It was all the same hum and nobody in the village had a word for it because you don't name the thing that holds you up.
>
> The shared hall smelled like yesterday's bread and tomorrow's soup. Senna was already there, arguing with a pot that had been disagreeable since before Brin was born. Torv sat in his usual chair, not eating yet, just sitting with his hands on the table like he was holding it down. Pell was in the granary. Pell was always in the granary. The stores were full. They would hold through winter. Pell checked again anyway.
>
> It was a good day. It was always a good day. That was the trouble with leaving — every day was good, and she'd have to remember them all without help.

### Academy (Immersion through contrast — Jordan-style)
> The training courtyard was paved in white stone. White stone that had never seen rain without being scrubbed, never seen mud without being mended, never seen a growing thing without someone deciding it didn't belong there. Brin's boots left marks. She was the only one — the other students wore enchanted shoes that repelled dust, or they floated an inch above the ground with a casual application of mana that Brin was fairly sure violated several principles of conservation but that nobody seemed to care about because it looked effortless.
>
> Around her, students drew circles in the air. Shimmering rings, three, four, five layers deep, each one humming at a different pitch as the chanters synchronized their voices. The sound bounced off the courtyard walls and came back layered — harmonics building on harmonics, the whole space vibrating with coordinated intent. It was beautiful. It was also completely useless for anything except looking beautiful, because the exercise was a display circle — a circle that did nothing except glow while its caster bowed.
>
> Brin watched a boy complete a five-ring display. The circles flared, the crowd applauded, the boy adjusted his collar and looked around for approval. The professor said "impressive" in a tone that meant it.
>
> Brin thought about the garden beds back home. The circles there did something every day and nobody said anything about it because the tomatoes didn't care about applause.

### Crisis (Immersion through sensory shift — Jordan-style)
> The Greywash had swallowed Thalmark. That was the only way to describe it — the river hadn't flooded the province, it had *eaten* it, taken the fields and the roads and the houses into itself and dissolved them into mud. Fields that should have been golden were brown water stretching to the horizon, broken only by the occasional roof or tree or fence post sticking up like a hand reaching for something it couldn't grab. Roads that should have carried carts carried boats, and the boats carried people who had been farmers yesterday and were refugees today and didn't have a word for what they were because the empire had never needed that word before.
>
> Brin knelt at the edge of what had been a farm. She could tell it had been a farm because the soil was wrong — not muddy-wrong but *structured*-wrong, packed and beaten into submission by years of drainage and levee work until it had forgotten how to breathe. She pressed her palm flat and felt nothing. No warmth. No pulse. No hum in the ground. The roots were drowned. The mycelium was dead. The soil had been dead long before the flood — the flood had just made it obvious.
>
> She drew a circle in the mud anyway. One ring. She hummed — not a song she chose, just the one that was there, the melody her grandmother taught her, the one that was older than the village and older than the empire and older than the river.
>
> The magic worked. Slowly. Like healing a burn on someone who had stopped believing in medicine. The wound in the soil closed, barely, the edges knitting together with a reluctance that had nothing to do with mana and everything to do with the fact that this ground had been forced into a shape it was never meant to hold.
>
> Beside her, a confederation engineer was rebuilding a levee with four other workers and no magic at all. They moved in silence — no chanting, no circles, just hands and tools and the kind of coordination that comes from doing a thing a thousand times until your body knows the rhythm. They were faster.

## Editing Checklist

Before any scene is considered done:

- [ ] Can I remove any sentence that *tells* the reader what to feel?
- [ ] Does every description serve at least two purposes?
- [ ] Does the POV character notice things consistent with their worldview?
- [ ] Is there at least one gap between what the character knows and what the reader knows?
- [ ] Does the dialogue sound like the specific character, not like "a character"?
- [ ] Is there a sensory anchor the reader can hold?
- [ ] Have I avoided naming emotions?
- [ ] Have I avoided explaining the magic system?
- [ ] Have I avoided tonal cuts / crystallizing lines / punchline structures?
- [ ] Does the prose flow — can the reader sink in and stay under?
- [ ] Does this scene earn its place — does it build character, advance plot, or deepen world?
- [ ] Would a reader get lost in this? Or would they get pulled out?
